## The three objects

Health in a working relationship is the *convergence* of three objects, not the alignment of a person to a role:

1. **Role discretion** — what the work actually demands. Not the job description; the discretionary space the work has to occupy for the outputs to be right.
2. **Discretion capacity** — what the person can handle, treated as trainable fitness rather than a fixed trait. What they've grown into so far, and what they could grow into if the org invested.
3. **Authority** — what the organization actually permits. The only one of the three that can change on Monday morning without either retraining a person or redefining a role.

The keystone insight came mid-build: **discretion capacity should match authority, not role discretion.** Match the person to what you've actually authorized them to do, not to the fantasy job description that may or may not be honored by the surrounding system. Role discretion is where the work wants to be. Authority is where the work is allowed to be. When those two are misaligned, the person is caught in a vise regardless of their capacity.

## The artifacts

Five documents split across two tracks.

**Public-facing** (the framework itself):

- The framing document, eleven sections, explains the three-object model and why the standard "role fit" language collapses meaningfully important distinctions.
- The Scoring Instrument — three indices, three anchored sub-questions each, hand-calibrated against four test cases. Mid-build, the C3 anchor (quality of judgment) was caught conflating volume of judgment with quality — no seat existed for the confident-but-poor decider. Re-anchored: 0 = "defers, does not decide," 1 = "decides, but unsoundly." The re-anchor is small in wording and load-bearing in effect.
- **What Management Was For** — a companion essay arguing that information asymmetry was the hidden organizing principle of the firm for a century, and that its erosion is what has been eating middle management for a decade. Explains why the three-object framework is arriving at exactly the moment when the historical fiction of "just do your job" is losing its structural support.

**Applied** (the tools):

- The Alignment POC — a manager's guided wizard for scoring an individual employee's fit strand by strand.
- The Six-Type Audit — an executive self-assessment tool. See below.

## The Six-Type Audit

The audit is a companion piece and, in practice, the artifact that has taught me the most about the framework it belongs to.

It asks a specific question of an executive at the end of a cycle: **did you personally do value-creating work, or did you do work that only looked like it?** The distinction matters because senior seats are structurally protected from feedback — the person auditing them is usually the person they report to, who has weaker information than the executive themselves about how the work went. Absent a self-audit, the seat drifts into performance without correction.

Six categories organize the audit:

1. **Value-stream work** — output the organization would have paid an outside party to produce. Real deliverables with external accountability.
2. **Stewardship** — decisions about people, capital allocation, or structural design that the executive was uniquely positioned to make and that would not have happened without them.
3. **Opinion** — considered positions offered to the organization that shaped decisions the executive didn't personally make. Includes strategy commentary, deal review, hiring input.
4. **Rehearsal** — preparation and practice for future value-stream, stewardship, or opinion work. Legitimate but easy to over-count.
5. **Performance** — visible activity that looked like work to observers but did not, on honest reflection, produce output, shape a decision, or prepare the executive for either. This is the category the audit is designed to surface.
6. **Recovery** — necessary decompression time. Not shameful, but must be named honestly rather than disguised as one of the others.

The executive fills out a rough time-share across the six categories for the cycle. A three-way split among value-stream work, stewardship, and opinion is the healthy pattern for a senior seat. Performance dominating any category is the honest signal that something has drifted. Recovery quietly dominating is a different signal worth attending to — usually the seat is being asked to sustain more than the person can carry.

The audit's value isn't its precision. It's that the exercise of *categorizing* the cycle exposes the seams the executive would otherwise be able to blur.

## State of play

Still on the grill because the **strand concept** — the observation that any real role is a bundle of distinct threads of work, each landing at different points on the discretion/authority/capacity space, and averaging across them is the instrument's core failure mode — is settled in principle but not yet wired into the scoring tool. That's the next piece of work.

Also queued: a shared vocabulary for the Six-Type Audit that translates cleanly across banking, tech, and professional services. Right now the category labels read fine in the finance context; other domains will want light adaptation without diluting the framework.

Related: Org Impact Mapper — both frameworks are about seeing an organizational role clearly rather than through the flattering fog of job descriptions. This one focuses on the individual-role fit; Impact Mapper focuses on what the role *is* in the larger system. They're complementary tools that got developed in the same period of thinking, and I use them together in practice.
