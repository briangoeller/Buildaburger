## The idea

Health in a working relationship is the *convergence* of three objects, not the alignment of a person to a role. The three:

1. **Role discretion** — what the work demands.
2. **Discretion capacity** — what the person can handle, treated as trainable fitness rather than a fixed trait.
3. **Authority** — what the organization permits. The only one of the three the organization can change on Monday morning.

The original keystone insight came mid-build: *discretion capacity should ultimately match authority*. Match the person to what you've actually authorized them to do, not to a job description that may or may not be honored in practice.

## What came together

Five artifacts split across two threads:

**Public-facing (the framework itself):**

- The Alignment of Judgment — an eleven-section framing document.
- The Scoring Instrument — a rubric with three indices of three anchored sub-questions each, hand-calibrated against four test cases.
- *What Management Was For* — an essay arguing that information asymmetry was the hidden organizing principle of the firm for a century, and is now eroding.

**Applied (the tools):**

- The Alignment POC — a manager's guided wizard for assessing an individual employee's strand-by-strand fit.
- The Self-Audit — for executives to assess whether they personally added value in a cycle, built around the six types of work and a three-way time split between value-stream work, stewardship, and opinion.

A mid-build instrument flaw was caught in calibration: the original C3 anchor (quality of judgment) conflated *volume* of judgment with *quality*, leaving no place for a confident-but-poor decider. Re-anchored so 0 = "defers, does not decide" and 1 = "decides, but unsoundly."

## State of play

Still on the grill because the **strand concept** — that any role is a bundle of distinct threads of work, each landing at different points on the discretion/span plane, and averaging across them is the instrument's core failure mode — is settled in principle but not yet wired into the scoring tool. That's the next move.
