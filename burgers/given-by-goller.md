## The idea

A handbag line built from Platonic geometry. Three bags — a cube, a sphere, a tetrahedron — whose silhouettes are the solids themselves, not interpretations of them. Patent leather in Bauhaus primary colors plus white and black. The integrated handle is a cutout of a *different* shape on each bag, in a closed three-way rotation: square bag, circle handle; circle bag, triangle handle; triangle bag, square handle.

## What came together

Bags are sold only as complete sets of three in a single colorway. Mixing colorways requires buying multiple sets. The mechanic enforces brand philosophy (excess and geometric completeness) and shapes economics simultaneously: fewer customers, higher unit value, no bargain entry point.

Distribution is deliberately unconventional. No website. No trade shows. No wholesale. Mystery, scarcity through announced limited annual runs, and a gallery-based acquisition ceremony where buyers collect their sets in person with formal display and wrapping. Markets are deliberately "second cities" — Chicago, Carmel-by-the-Sea, Boston — as an inversion of luxury's New York / London / Tokyo default.

The name went from *Oligarch* (working title, deliberately provocative, not a real candidate) to **Given by Göller**, restoring the German spelling of the family name.

## State of play

Tech Pack v1.1 exists, with the sphere flagged as the highest manufacturing risk (patent leather over a compound curve). A Blender Python script renders all three forms with mathematically correct vertex coordinates, boolean handle cutouts, patent shader materials, three-point product lighting, and a hero camera angle.

Eight open design decisions are still listed in the tech pack, ranging from closure mechanism to interior lining stitch pattern. The phone number as the sole contact mechanism is decided. The first seed recipient is decided.

What isn't decided: whether this ever becomes a real business or remains a portfolio piece. The economics work at low volume; the question is whether the gallery-ceremony distribution model holds together when it leaves the page.
