## The question

The 72 seals of the Lesser Key of Solomon are visual glyphs designed by (probably) sixteenth or seventeenth-century occultists to represent specific entities. They are pretty. They are also, on inspection, structurally consistent in odd ways — the kind of consistency that might just be aesthetic convention, and might be something else.

The question is whether they encode extractable information under standard image-analysis transforms. Not "do they summon anything" — that isn't the question. The question is whether the humans who designed them may have encoded information deliberately or accidentally that shows up under modern analysis.

## The first test

Asymmetry-distribution as the opening move. Do the seals cluster on any measurable geometric axis at rates above chance? Are their bounding shapes, stroke densities, or symmetry-breaking directions distributed in a way that suggests non-aesthetic patterning? If yes, that's a signal worth following into Fourier-domain analysis and edge-density comparisons. If no, they're beautiful and structurally uninteresting, and the investigation ends there without embarrassment.

## State of play

Pipeline designed but not built. A clean image set of the seals is being assembled — the tricky part is that the standard published sources all have different renderings that introduce visual noise, and separating scribal drift from designed structure requires source-critical work before the analysis can even begin. First test not yet run.
