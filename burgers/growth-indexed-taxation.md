## The idea

Federal income tax tiered not by income, but by the **economic multiplier of the industry producing it**. Foundational industries with high spillover (manufacturing, agriculture, construction) sit near the bottom of the rate schedule. Speculative financial services with low multipliers sit at the top. Overall average target around 29%, with the structure doing the work of progressivity rather than rate-bracket whiplash inside a single household's return.

## What came together

The framework started as an SIC-code thought experiment and evolved into something stricter: a five-tier schedule anchored to BEA Type II industry accounts, with a secondary passive-income schedule pinned to a Tier 3 fulcrum. The revenue model was built with explicit uncertainty ranges rather than a single point estimate — a discipline that turned out to make the whole thing easier to defend.

Two writing decisions mattered. First, the worker-equity and ownership-cap material was pulled out into a separate future document so the tax paper could keep its focus. Second, a dedicated rebuttal section on the nationalization objection was added — that objection was the single most common pushback in informal review, and it deserved its own treatment rather than a paragraph buried in the conclusion.

## State of play

Three deliverables exist: the full white paper, the nationalization rebuttal supplement, and an interactive revenue-model widget. Publication plan is Substack → GitHub → LinkedIn cross-post → self-published book, in that order, with the lattice serving as a foundation for the book rather than something to be retired by it.

The known weakness: this is a policy proposal, not a forecast. Static-scoring revenue numbers tell you what the math says assuming no behavioral response; dynamic-scoring versions would tell you what humans would actually do. The current paper handles this by stating the assumption explicitly rather than pretending to model it.
