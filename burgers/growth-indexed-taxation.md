## The problem worth naming

The U.S. federal income tax has a single organizing variable: the magnitude of income earned in a year. Brackets sort taxpayers by how much they make. Rates rise as income rises. Deductions and credits modify the math at the margins, but they don't displace the central principle. The code asks how much you earned, and assigns a rate accordingly.

This has a quiet consequence that almost never gets named directly. The tax code communicates nothing about *where* economic activity should occur. It is allocation-blind. A dollar earned in semiconductor manufacturing is taxed identically to a dollar earned in high-frequency arbitrage of the same magnitude. To the federal tax system, they are the same transaction.

The framework proposed here takes the opposite position. It treats the question of *where* economic activity occurs as the primary signal the tax code should send. The mechanism is straightforward: an industry's effective tax rate is indexed to its measured economic output multiplier. Industries whose activity produces large downstream effects are taxed at low or even negative rates. Industries whose activity produces little or negative downstream effect are taxed at high rates.

The resulting structure converts the tax code into an allocation instrument.

## The framework

Five tiers, indexed to the BEA Type II output multiplier — an established, publicly maintained dataset that already exists. The Bureau of Economic Analysis updates these multipliers annually and publishes them transparently. The framework just borrows them.

| Tier | Multiplier | Representative industries | Operational rate | Passive rate |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **1** | ≥ 1.8 | Agriculture, energy infrastructure, primary manufacturing | 0% | 14.5% |
| **2** | 1.5 – 1.8 | Construction, secondary manufacturing | 18% | 23.5% |
| **3** | 1.2 – 1.5 | Retail, wholesale, professional services *(fulcrum)* | 29% | 29% |
| **4** | 1.0 – 1.2 | Real estate, traditional financial services | 45% | 37% |
| **5** | < 1.0 | Speculative finance, derivatives, proprietary trading | 68% | 48.5% |

Two things to notice. First, **Tier 1 can go negative.** Through refundable credits applied at the tier level, the effective rate on the most productive activity in the economy can drop below zero — the government paying farmers and primary manufacturers to operate, in the same way it already pays homeowners through the mortgage interest deduction and corporations through the R&D credit. (The objection that this implies nationalization is the load-bearing political pushback; the [supplementary section](https://github.com/briangoeller/Growth-Tax-Model/blob/main/Growth-Indexed-Taxation-Supplementary.docx) handles it directly.)

Second, **passive income is treated separately**, through what I call the *fulcrum mechanism.* The passive rate for income generated by a given tier is the midpoint between that tier's operational rate and the Tier 3 fulcrum of 29%. The schedule converges toward the central tier from both directions: passive income from Tier 1 activity is taxed *above* the operational rate (14.5% vs. 0%), and passive income from Tier 5 activity is taxed *below* the operational rate (48.5% vs. 68%). The fulcrum acts as a structural counterweight that keeps the operational schedule's rate differentials from producing arbitrarily large preferences or punishments in the passive space.

## The revenue case

A revenue model at FY2023 baseline (GDP $27.4T, taxable share 65%) projects approximately **$5.63 trillion gross** at the default tier rates, against current federal receipts of $4.44 trillion. After a 10% avoidance drag — conservative relative to the 15–25% experienced by other high-rate systems — net revenue projects to approximately **$5.07 trillion**, or roughly **+14% above baseline**.

The model carries a stated uncertainty range of approximately **±30% at the 90% confidence level.** The honest claim isn't that the framework raises a specific amount of additional revenue. The honest claim is that it raises revenue at all while simultaneously producing a capital allocation signal the current code lacks. The structural argument operates independently of the revenue point estimate.

You can play with the revenue model directly — adjust any tier rate, move the fulcrum, change the avoidance drag, and watch the projection recompute live. The model is hosted [here](https://github.com/briangoeller/Growth-Tax-Model/blob/main/revenue-model.html).

## The structural innovation

The most innovative element of the framework isn't the tier structure or the fulcrum. It's the **annual recalibration mechanic.**

Unlike a conventional tax rate, which is set by statute and requires new legislation to change, the GITS tier rates would be set within statutory ranges by an independent technical body — modeled on the Federal Reserve's Open Market Committee — using updated BEA multiplier data and a transparent methodology. The statutory ranges remain congressional; the calibration within them moves annually.

This converts the tax schedule from a static code provision into a dynamic fiscal policy instrument. The recalibrating body would, in effect, signal where the government wants economic energy to flow in the coming year — lowering Tier 2 rates to encourage domestic manufacturing investment, raising Tier 4 rates to dampen commercial real estate speculation — without requiring new legislation for each adjustment. The instrument is analogous in function to the federal funds rate: a single adjustable variable that encodes enormous amounts of economic policy intent and communicates it simultaneously to every market participant.

A federal funds rate for the tax code. That's the closest one-line summary of what the framework is for.

## What the documents are

The full case lives across three artifacts, linked below:

- **The white paper** — five parts, working-paper format, sets out the framework, the passive schedule, the revenue model with its uncertainty bounds, and the implementation discussion (political reality, anti-avoidance, phase-in, constitutional considerations).
- **The supplementary section** — a standalone rebuttal of the nationalization objection, separated out because that objection requires direct treatment in language that travels in political conversations the working paper won't reach.
- **The revenue model** — an interactive HTML companion that lets you adjust any input and see how it propagates.

The framework is offered as a working paper. The tier rates, multiplier ranges, base estimates, and avoidance assumptions are defaults illustrating the structure, not recommendations fixing it. What the paper claims to contribute is the architecture: the choice of multiplier as the organizing variable, the fulcrum design, the recalibration mechanic, and the anti-avoidance architecture. Critique and refinement are exactly the point.

## What's deliberately not here yet

The framework grew out of a conversation that also produced a worker-equity and ownership-cap proposal. It was pulled out of this paper to preserve focus, and is reserved for a separate future document. The reason is that the equity proposal answers a *different* question — how should productive surplus be distributed within firms — than the question this paper answers, which is how should the tax code allocate economic activity across industries. Combining them produced a paper that did neither well.

The two are companion pieces. They will eventually be released as such. They are not the same paper.
