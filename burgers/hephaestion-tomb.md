## The premise

A group of friends decide, in youth, to hold a lifelong contest. The one who lived the greatest life will have a statue raised in their honor after they all pass. The book ends without telling the reader who won. Reader must judge.

## The title

When Hephaestion died, Alexander petitioned the oracle to have him named a god. The oracle refused. Alexander was permitted only to name him *hero*. Mortal greatness acknowledged; immortality denied.

The oracle was doing philosophy. There is a category for what humans achieve at their best and it isn't divinity. It's something more specific and more honest — heroic, fully human, fully mortal, fully real. The friends in the story are running the same petition Alexander ran. The reader is running the oracle's answer.

## The scoring the characters don't know they're being judged on

Jungian archetypes and individuation. Not conventional greatness — not wealth, not achievement, not legacy. Depth of becoming. Who lived most fully into their shadow. Who integrated the anima or animus. Who reached genuine self rather than performed self. Who arrived at the ground state without flinching.

The characters cannot see this. Only the reader can. Greek dramatic irony is the mechanism.

## State of play

Long-standing. Roughed out on paper years ago. Structure: dual-timeline, contemporary friends intercut with the ancient story of Hephaestion's final months.

Sits in the puzzle space, held there deliberately, because the moment I force the connection to the Siderophile burger is probably the moment both stories die. They ask the same question at different scales — Siderophile at civilization scale, Hephaestion's Tomb at single-life scale — and the iridium and the statue are the same object. But held together on the page they collapse into thesis. Held separately they remain stories.
