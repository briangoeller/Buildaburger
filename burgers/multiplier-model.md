## The core claim

Tools don't democratize skill. They amplify the distribution. **Skill × Tool = Output** — which means a more powerful tool widens the gap between skilled and unskilled users rather than closing it. The 1980s synthesizer panic, the calculator panic, the desktop-publishing panic, and now the AI panic are all the same pattern reading itself wrong. The tool doesn't collapse the hierarchy; it magnifies it.

The evidence keeps recurring in exactly the shape the model predicts. Give the same synthesizer to two musicians and the difference in output grows, not shrinks. Give the same spreadsheet to two analysts and the same thing happens. The tool is a multiplier over skill, not a substitute for it, and the fear of substitution has been getting the direction of the effect wrong for forty years.

## The endpoint principle

The framework crystallized around a sharper claim than "everyone has to add value": **everyone has to be an expert on at least one endpoint.** Tools automate the *middle* of any transformation. Expertise means owning the input or the output. The wider the middle column a tool can handle, the more valuable endpoint expertise becomes — not less.

This lands the consulting reframe cleanly. Consultants have always been imported endpoint expertise, compensating for managers too consumed by middle-column execution to develop input and output discipline themselves. The same logic applies in-house: when AI handles the middle, the question isn't whether your people will be replaced. The question is whether they own an endpoint anyone is paying for. If they don't, the tool's multiplier catches them from the wrong side. If they do, the multiplier compounds their value.

## The row model

The endpoint principle extends across institutional roles when you widen the frame. Any organization or industry can be described as a sequence of columns:

> **trigger event → discovery → institutional transformation → trajectory → flourishing outcome**

Different roles own different columns. The person who identifies the trigger event owns the input endpoint. The person who shapes the flourishing outcome owns the output endpoint. Everyone in between is operating in the middle columns, which is exactly the space tools chew through fastest.

The consequential observation for legacy companies: **the middle column often *is* the IP.** Banks live there. So do insurance companies. So do most established professional-services firms. Their institutional value has accumulated in the machinery of the transformation itself — the workflows, the compliance architecture, the risk-adjusted processes, the trained personnel who know how to route the middle-column work. When the tool can do the middle, the IP starts revaluing.

Where the endpoint principle points at the individual level, the row model points at the institutional level. Companies whose competitive position is anchored in the middle column need to be developing input or output ownership right now, because the tool that eats their middle is not two years away. It's shipping quarterly. The move is to migrate institutional value from *how well we execute the middle* to *how well we identify the trigger* or *how well we shape the outcome*. Those are the sustainable positions. The middle is not.

The row model also has a subtler use: it makes visible the alliances that survive the multiplier. Trigger-owners and outcome-owners need each other in a way that middle-column operators never quite needed either. A financial advisor whose value is asset allocation execution loses to the tool; a financial advisor whose value is understanding the client's life-goal (input) and shaping the estate distribution (output) is a person the tool amplifies, not replaces.
