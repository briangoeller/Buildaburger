## The idea

Tools don't democratize skill. They amplify the distribution. **Skill × Tool = Output** — which means a more powerful tool widens the gap between skilled and unskilled users rather than closing it. The 1980s synthesizer panic, the calculator panic, the desktop-publishing panic, and now the AI panic are all the same pattern reading itself wrong.

## What came together

The framework crystallized around a sharper claim than "everyone has to add value": **everyone has to be an expert on at least one endpoint.** Tools automate the *middle* of any transformation. Expertise means owning the input or the output. The wider the middle column a tool can handle, the more valuable endpoint expertise becomes — not less.

This led to the consulting reframe: consultants have always been imported endpoint expertise, compensating for managers too consumed by middle-column execution to develop input and output discipline themselves. The reframe lands the same logic on the in-house side: when AI handles the middle, the question isn't whether your people will be replaced. The question is whether they own an endpoint anyone is paying for.

## State of play

There's a "row model" that extends this across institutional roles — trigger event → discovery → institutional transformation → trajectory → flourishing outcome — with the consequence that for legacy companies, the middle column often *is* the IP. **Banks live in the middle column.** That's the load-bearing observation for everything else I do at PremierBank.
