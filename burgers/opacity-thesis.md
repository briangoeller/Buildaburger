## The idea

Agency, intelligence, and choice are not properties that systems *have*. They are attributions observers make into gaps in their own visibility of a system's mechanism. When the mechanism is opaque enough that a pattern's origin can't be traced, "something is deciding" is the story that fills the gap. The claim isn't that agency doesn't exist — it's that what we call agency-attribution tracks mechanism-visibility rather than actual agency, and does so systematically.

## The Light Switch Test

The demonstration is a three-flipper thought experiment. A human flips a light switch. A lightning-driven device flips a light switch. An AI model flips a light switch. Observers watching the resulting pattern of on-and-off states will attribute "choice" to the human immediately, to the AI equivocally, and to the lightning-driven device not at all — despite the lightning source, which everyone agrees has no agency, producing patterns fully as complex and unpredictable as the other two. "Looks chosen" is decoupled from "is choosing." The variable that actually determines the attribution is how visible the mechanism is to the observer.

The mathematical grounding is Chaitin's algorithmic information theory: whether a string is structured or random is undecidable in the general case. This is not a claim about our present ignorance — it's a formal proof that the door cannot be opened even in principle. Applied to the agency question, it says the observer's inability to distinguish "chosen pattern" from "structured randomness" is a mathematical property of the situation, not a temporary state of knowledge.

The framework extends naturally to a few other domains where the same confusion is running unresolved: the assembly-theory debate in astrobiology (is high assembly complexity evidence of life, or of something we don't understand yet?), SETI signal detection (structured versus random extraterrestrial signals face the same undecidability), and Gödelian self-reference more generally. Signal-versus-noise turns out not to be a feature of the world but a relation between the world and an embedded observer who cannot exit to verify the classification.

## State of play

Grill because nothing has been shipped yet. An edit pass on the canonical document is queued before publication. The one substantive open question I want to think about more before pushing to LessWrong: whether the mathematical grounding claim is being stated at the correct strength. Chaitin proves that structure and randomness are undecidable in the general case. What that does *not* prove is that intent doesn't exist. It proves that structure and intent, from the outside, are uncertifiable. The philosophical bridge from "uncertifiable" to "attribution-not-property" is my own contribution and I want to make sure the paper marks the seam clearly rather than smuggling it across.
