## The idea

Two questions define where a person sits in an organization: **how much impact does their work have outside the company** — customers, markets, regulators, the shape of the industry — and **how much leverage do they carry inside it** — access to resources, hiring authority, veto power, the ability to reshape workflows without asking permission.

Put those on two axes and four quadrants fall out. Every seat in an organization sits in one of them, and the character of the seat — what it's for, who's good at it, how it should be compensated, when it should be moved — follows from which quadrant it's in.

## The four quadrants

**Catalyst** *(high external impact, low internal leverage)*. The maverick. Drives change without formal authority. The technical fellow whose ideas rewrite the roadmap; the salesperson whose relationships determine the pipeline; the writer whose external presence shapes how the company is seen. Catalysts run on reputation and craft. They're expensive to retain because they don't need the org to do their work — they need the org to fund their work — and if the org stops being useful to them, they leave without much friction. Their compensation ceiling is external, not internal.

**Rainmaker** *(high external impact, high internal leverage)*. Brings in the deals, the customers, or the relationships that fund the rest of the organization *and* has the internal power to allocate what they bring. Senior client partners at consulting firms. Franchise founders. The rare executive who both closes the biggest deals and controls the org's investment strategy. Rainmakers are almost always the highest-compensated seats in a firm, and their departure destabilizes it — they take both the revenue and the political center of gravity with them.

**Linchpin** *(low external impact, high internal leverage)*. The person who makes the org actually function. Head of ops. GC. The senior engineer who knows how the legacy systems talk to each other. Linchpins are irreplaceable operationally and invisible externally; they hold the org together in ways that don't show up in press releases. Their compensation is often less than a Rainmaker's despite carrying comparable systemic risk to their loss. When a Linchpin leaves unexpectedly, the org discovers what they were holding up — usually by having it fall over.

**Sustainer** *(low external impact, low internal leverage)*. Necessary work. Replaceable people. This is the largest quadrant by headcount and the hardest one to name honestly, because everybody's job description says they're a Linchpin. Most aren't. Sustainer isn't an insult — it's a description of a seat, and most seats in most companies are Sustainer seats. The framework's honesty depends on being willing to say so.

## Why it matters

Three uses fall out of the taxonomy:

**Executive self-assessment.** Where do you actually sit? The answer determines what your job is *for* — Rainmakers should be closing, Linchpins should be operating, Catalysts should be provoking, Sustainers should be executing — and the failure mode of each seat is trying to do the work of a different quadrant.

**Team composition.** A team of all Catalysts produces exciting ideas that never ship. A team of all Sustainers ships mediocrely and consistently. A team of all Linchpins never notices the world outside the walls. The interesting teams are quadrant-mixed on purpose.

**Investment and redeployment decisions.** The question "where should we spend our marginal management attention?" has different answers depending on which quadrant you're trying to grow. Catalysts want investment in external platform. Rainmakers want investment in deal capacity. Linchpins want investment in systems that reduce single-point-of-failure risk. Sustainers want investment in process clarity. Confusing these produces wasted spend.

## State of play

Framework crystallized. Axes defined, quadrants named, characterization of each quadrant stable across stress-tests. The scoring instrument doesn't exist yet — that's the grill piece. What it wants: a rubric that lets a manager score a seat honestly against the two axes without the "everybody's a Linchpin" flattery bias I mentioned above. Probably three or four anchored questions per axis, each with concrete behavioral anchors rather than adjectival ones.

Related to the Alignment of Judgment burger — both frameworks are about seeing an organizational role clearly rather than through the flattering fog of job descriptions. Alignment focuses on the individual-role fit; Impact Mapper focuses on what the role *is* in the larger system. They're complementary tools.
