## The premise

Working title: *Siderophile*. Iron-loving. The elements that sink toward the core.

What if a technological civilization existed on Earth 66 million years ago and left the iridium layer as its extinction signature? Iridium is unusually concentrated at the K-Pg boundary and is conventionally attributed to the Chicxulub asteroid impact. Alternative reading: an advanced civilization's collapse could leave a similar chemical signature. Not a claim about what actually happened; a premise for a specific piece of speculative fiction.

## The structure

Dual timeline. The ancient civilization's story unfolds in parallel with the story of a modern scientist studying siderophile elements in the K-Pg layer millions of years later. Neither character knows the other exists. The reader holds both.

The scientist is a siderophile herself — a self that loves its own iron, its own weight, its own tendency to sink toward the core of something she can't name. She's studying siderophile elements professionally and she is one existentially, and she doesn't see the connection. The reader does. The tehom moves beneath both timelines.

Greek tragedy shape: the ancient civilization's fate is already sealed before the reader meets them. What the story is *for* is what they do inside the constraint. It ends with an ordinary moment in each timeline, happening simultaneously, neither character knowing the other exists.

## State of play

Not going to be a book. The exercise is the interesting part. Overlaps deliberately with the Hephaestion's Tomb burger — same question at different scales. Siderophile asks what an entire civilization amounts to when the wave collapses. Hephaestion's Tomb asks what a single life amounts to. Same question. Different rendering frames. The iridium and the statue are the same object — the trace at the boundary.

Held in the puzzle space on purpose. Forcing the connection is probably what kills both stories.
